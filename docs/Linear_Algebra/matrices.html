Matrices

Back to Linear Algebra Topics

Matrices are a fundamental concept in linear algebra and are used to represent and manipulate data in various fields including mathematics, physics, and engineering.

Definition

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns. A matrix with $m$ rows and $n$ columns is called an $m \times n$ matrix. Each number in the matrix is called an element.

Example of a $2 \times 3$ matrix:

$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$

Matrix Operations

Common operations on matrices include addition, subtraction, and multiplication.

Addition and Subtraction

Two matrices of the same dimension can be added or subtracted element-wise. If $A$ and $B$ are both $m \times n$ matrices, their sum $C = A + B$ is also an $m \times n$ matrix with elements:

$c_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}$

Example:

$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 8 & 9 \\ 10 & 11 & 12 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 & 10 & 12 \\ 14 & 16 & 18 \end{pmatrix}$

Matrix Multiplication

The product of an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ and an $n \times p$ matrix $B$ is an $m \times p$ matrix $C = AB$. The element $c_{ij}$ of $C$ is computed as:

$c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} b_{kj}$

Example:

$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 7 & 1 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 8 \\ 3 \cdot 5 + 4 \cdot 7 & 3 \cdot 6 + 4 \cdot 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}$

Transpose of a Matrix

The transpose of a matrix $A$, denoted by $A^T$, is obtained by swapping the rows and columns of $A$. If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix, then $A^T$ is an $n \times m$ matrix.

Example:

$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$

Determinant of a Matrix

The determinant is a scalar value that can be computed from a square matrix and encapsulates some of its properties. For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$:

$\text{det}(A) = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc$

Example:

$\text{det}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = 1 \cdot 4 - 2 \cdot 3 = 4 - 6 = -2$

Inverse of a Matrix

The inverse of a square matrix $A$ is a matrix $A^{-1}$ such that $AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I$, where $I$ is the identity matrix. Not all matrices have inverses; a matrix must be square and have a non-zero determinant to be invertible.

Example for a $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\text{det}(A)} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$

For $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$:

$\text{det}(A) = -2$, $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-2} \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1.5 & -0.5 \end{pmatrix}$

Special Types of Matrices

There are several special types of matrices, including:

  • Identity Matrix: A square matrix with ones on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere.
  • Diagonal Matrix: A matrix with non-zero elements only on the diagonal.
  • Orthogonal Matrix: A square matrix whose rows and columns are orthogonal unit vectors.
  • Symmetric Matrix: A matrix that is equal to its transpose, $A = A^T$.

Conclusion

Matrices are essential tools in linear algebra with applications in various scientific and engineering disciplines. Understanding their properties and operations is fundamental for solving complex problems in these fields.
