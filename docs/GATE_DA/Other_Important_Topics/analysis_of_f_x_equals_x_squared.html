Analysis of \( f(x) = x^2 \)

Domain and Range

The domain of \( f \) is \( \mathbb{R} \) (all real numbers) since any real number \( x \) can be squared.
The range of \( f \) is \( [0, \infty) \) (all non-negative real numbers) because \( x^2 \) is always non-negative and can take any value greater than or equal to 0.

Function Properties

Even Function: \( f \) is an even function because \( f(-x) = (-x)^2 = x^2 = f(x) \).
Continuous: \( f \) is continuous for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) because the polynomial function \( x^2 \) is continuous everywhere.
Differentiable: \( f \) is differentiable for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \). The derivative \( f'(x) = 2x \) is defined everywhere on \( \mathbb{R} \).

Critical Points and Extrema

To find the critical points, set the derivative equal to zero: \( f'(x) = 2x = 0 \) gives \( x = 0 \).
At \( x = 0 \), \( f(0) = 0^2 = 0 \).
This is a minimum point because the second derivative \( f''(x) = 2 \) is positive, indicating a local minimum.

Behavior at Infinity

As \( x \to \infty \), \( f(x) = x^2 \to \infty \).
As \( x \to -\infty \), \( f(x) = x^2 \to \infty \).

Graph of the Function

The graph of \( f(x) = x^2 \) is a parabola opening upwards with its vertex at the origin (0,0).

Injectivity

A function is injective if different inputs produce different outputs, i.e., if \( f(x_1) = f(x_2) \) implies \( x_1 = x_2 \).
For \( f(x) = x^2 \):
If \( f(x_1) = f(x_2) \), then \( x_1^2 = x_2^2 \).
This implies \( x_1 = x_2 \) or \( x_1 = -x_2 \).
Since \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \) can be different (one positive and one negative) but produce the same output, \( f \) is not injective.
For example, \( f(2) = 4 \) and \( f(-2) = 4 \).

Surjectivity

A function is surjective if every element in the codomain has a preimage in the domain, i.e., for every \( y \in \mathbb{R} \), there exists an \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) such that \( f(x) = y \).
For \( f(x) = x^2 \):
The codomain (range) we consider here is \( \mathbb{R} \).
However, \( x^2 \) is always non-negative, meaning \( f(x) \geq 0 \).
Therefore, \( f(x) = x^2 \) maps \( \mathbb{R} \) to \([0, \infty)\), not the entire set of real numbers \( \mathbb{R} \).
There are no \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) such that \( f(x) = -1 \) or any negative number. Hence, \( f \) is not surjective when considering the codomain as \( \mathbb{R} \).

Graphical Analysis

Graph of \( f(x) = x^2 \):
The graph is a parabola that opens upwards with its vertex at the origin \((0, 0)\).

Horizontal Line Test for Injectivity:
Draw horizontal lines at various levels (e.g., \( y = 1, y = 4 \)).
Observe that these lines intersect the graph at two points for \( y > 0 \).
This shows that the function is not injective.

Horizontal Line Test for Surjectivity:
Draw horizontal lines at various levels.
Horizontal lines at \( y = -1, y = -2 \) do not intersect the graph at all, indicating the function is not surjective for the entire set of real numbers.
For horizontal lines at \( y \geq 0 \), every line intersects the graph at least once, indicating the function is surjective for the non-negative real numbers.

Summary

Using the graph of \( f(x) = x^2 \):
Not Injective: The horizontal line test shows that horizontal lines intersect the graph at more than one point.
Not Surjective for \( \mathbb{R} \): There are no intersections for horizontal lines with negative \( y \)-values.
Surjective for \([0, \infty)\): Every non-negative horizontal line intersects the graph at least once.
