Sample Space

Back to Probability and Statistics Topics

In probability theory, the sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Each outcome is an element of the sample space, and the sample space is denoted by \( S \) or \( \Omega \).

Example

Consider the experiment of tossing a coin. The sample space is:

\[ S = \{ \text{Heads}, \text{Tails} \} \]

For rolling a six-sided die, the sample space is:

\[ S = \{ 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 \} \]

Types of Sample Spaces

  • Discrete Sample Space: Contains a finite or countably infinite number of outcomes. Examples include the outcomes of rolling a die or tossing a coin.
  • Continuous Sample Space: Contains an uncountably infinite number of outcomes. Examples include the possible outcomes of measuring the height of a person or the time taken to complete a task.

Events

An event is a subset of the sample space. For example, in the experiment of rolling a die, the event of rolling an even number is:

\[ E = \{ 2, 4, 6 \} \]

Union of Events

The union of two events \( A \) and \( B \), denoted by \( A \cup B \), is the set of outcomes that belong to either \( A \) or \( B \) or both. If \( A \) and \( B \) are events, then:

\[ A \cup B = \{ x : x \in A \text{ or } x \in B \} \]

Intersection of Events

The intersection of two events \( A \) and \( B \), denoted by \( A \cap B \), is the set of outcomes that belong to both \( A \) and \( B \). If \( A \) and \( B \) are events, then:

\[ A \cap B = \{ x : x \in A \text{ and } x \in B \} \]

Complement of an Event

The complement of an event \( A \), denoted by \( A' \) or \( A^c \), is the set of outcomes in the sample space that are not in \( A \). If \( A \) is an event, then:

\[ A' = \{ x : x \in S \text{ and } x \notin A \} \]

Applications

  • Probability Calculation: Sample spaces are used to define probabilities of events.
  • Statistical Analysis: Sample spaces form the basis for conducting statistical experiments and analyses.
  • Risk Assessment: Sample spaces are used to model possible outcomes and assess risks in various fields such as finance and engineering.

Conclusion

The concept of sample space is fundamental to probability theory and statistics. It provides a framework for defining and analyzing events, calculating probabilities, and conducting experiments.
