Permutations and Combinations

Back to Probability and Statistics Topics

Permutations

Permutations refer to the arrangement of objects in a specific order. The number of permutations of \( n \) distinct objects taken \( r \) at a time is given by:

\[ P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n - r)!} \]

where \( n! \) (n factorial) is the product of all positive integers up to \( n \).

Example

Consider 3 distinct objects: A, B, and C. The number of permutations of these 3 objects taken 2 at a time is:

\[ P(3, 2) = \frac{3!}{(3 - 2)!} = \frac{3!}{1!} = 6 \]

The permutations are: AB, BA, AC, CA, BC, and CB.

Combinations

Combinations refer to the selection of objects without regard to the order. The number of combinations of \( n \) distinct objects taken \( r \) at a time is given by:

\[ C(n, r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n - r)!} \]

Example

Consider 3 distinct objects: A, B, and C. The number of combinations of these 3 objects taken 2 at a time is:

\[ C(3, 2) = \binom{3}{2} = \frac{3!}{2!(3 - 2)!} = \frac{3!}{2! \cdot 1!} = 3 \]

The combinations are: AB, AC, and BC.

Applications

  • Permutations: Used in scenarios where the order of arrangement is important, such as seating arrangements, rankings, and schedules.
  • Combinations: Used in scenarios where the order of selection is not important, such as lottery draws, team selections, and combinations of flavors.

Conclusion

Understanding permutations and combinations is essential for solving problems related to counting and arrangement. These concepts are fundamental in probability and statistics, as well as in various real-world applications.
