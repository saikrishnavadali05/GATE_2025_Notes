Continuous Distributions

Back to Probability and Statistics Topics

Continuous distributions are probability distributions that describe the likelihood of a continuous random variable taking on a range of values. Unlike discrete distributions, which deal with countable outcomes, continuous distributions deal with uncountable outcomes, often represented as intervals on the real number line.

Key Continuous Distributions

There are several important continuous probability distributions commonly used in statistics:

Uniform Distribution

The uniform distribution is characterized by having equal probability for all values within a specified range \([a, b]\). The probability density function (PDF) is given by:

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{b - a} \quad \text{for} \quad a \leq x \leq b \]

Normal Distribution

The normal distribution, also known as the Gaussian distribution, is a symmetric distribution characterized by its bell-shaped curve. It is defined by its mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\). The PDF is given by:

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \]

Exponential Distribution

The exponential distribution describes the time between events in a Poisson process. It is characterized by the rate parameter \(\lambda\). The PDF is given by:

\[ f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \quad \text{for} \quad x \geq 0 \]

Log-Normal Distribution

The log-normal distribution describes a variable whose logarithm is normally distributed. It is characterized by the parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\). The PDF is given by:

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{x\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(\ln x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \quad \text{for} \quad x > 0 \]

Chi-Squared Distribution

The chi-squared distribution is used in hypothesis testing and confidence interval estimation for variance. It is characterized by the degrees of freedom \(k\). The PDF is given by:

\[ f(x) = \frac{1}{2^{k/2} \Gamma(k/2)} x^{k/2 - 1} e^{-x/2} \quad \text{for} \quad x \geq 0 \]

T-Distribution

The t-distribution is used in estimating the mean of a normally distributed population when the sample size is small. It is characterized by the degrees of freedom \( \nu \). The PDF is given by:

\[ f(x) = \frac{\Gamma((\nu+1)/2)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi} \Gamma(\nu/2)} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2} \]

Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a continuous random variable \(X\) gives the probability that \(X\) will take a value less than or equal to \(x\). It is defined as:

\[ F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt \]

Properties of Continuous Distributions

  • Probability Density Function (PDF): Describes the likelihood of a random variable taking on a specific value.
  • Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF): Describes the probability that a random variable will take a value less than or equal to a specific value.
  • Mean: The average or expected value of the distribution.
  • Variance: Measures the spread or dispersion of the distribution.
  • Standard Deviation: The square root of the variance, representing the average distance from the mean.

Applications

  • Normal Distribution: Used in natural and social sciences to represent real-valued random variables whose distributions are not known.
  • Exponential Distribution: Applied in queuing theory, reliability analysis, and survival analysis.
  • Log-Normal Distribution: Used to model stock prices and other financial data.
  • Chi-Squared Distribution: Used in hypothesis testing, particularly in tests of independence and goodness of fit.
  • T-Distribution: Applied in estimating population parameters when sample sizes are small.

Conclusion

Continuous distributions play a vital role in probability theory and statistics, providing models for real-valued random variables. Understanding these distributions and their properties is essential for data analysis, hypothesis testing, and predictive modeling.
